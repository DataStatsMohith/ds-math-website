Topic  ·  Information Theory

The Language
of Uncertainty

Information theory answers a deceptively simple question: how do you measure information? Its answers — entropy, KL divergence, mutual information — turn out to underpin the loss functions, training objectives, and evaluation metrics of almost every ML model ever built.

Why does a data scientist need this?
Cross-entropy loss (used in every classification model) is information theory. KL divergence is what VAEs minimise. Mutual information drives feature selection and representation learning. Decision trees split on information gain. RLHF uses KL penalties. You're already using this every day — now understand why it works.

Concept 01 / 04
🎲Entropy — Measuring Surprise

Entropy measures the average amount of surprise (or uncertainty) in a probability distribution. A fair coin flip has high entropy — you genuinely don't know what's coming. A biased coin that always lands heads has zero entropy — no surprise at all.

The key insight: rare events carry more information than common ones. "The sun rose this morning" tells you nothing. "Your model got 100% accuracy" is surprising — and informative. Claude Shannon formalised this in 1948 with $-\log_2 p$ bits of information for an event with probability $p$.

💡 The Coin Flip Intuition
A fair coin (p=0.5) gives 1 bit of entropy — you need exactly 1 binary question to determine the outcome. A 4-sided die gives 2 bits. A distribution where one event is certain gives 0 bits — you already know the answer, so no information is gained. Drag the slider below and watch entropy peak at maximum uncertainty.

🤖 In Data Science
Decision trees use information gain (reduction in entropy) to choose splits. A good split creates child nodes that are more "pure" (lower entropy). The Gini impurity used in sklearn is a close approximation to entropy. High entropy in your model's output distribution means it's uncertain — useful for detecting out-of-distribution samples.
📐 Shannon Entropy
$$H(X) = -\sum_{x} p(x)\log_2 p(x) = \mathbb{E}[-\log_2 p(X)]$$
$$\text{Binary: } H(p) = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p)$$

Joint entropy $H(X,Y) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log p(x,y)$ and conditional entropy $H(Y|X) = H(X,Y) - H(X)$. The chain rule: $H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X)$. Knowing X can only reduce (or maintain) the entropy of Y — it cannot increase it: $H(Y|X) \leq H(Y)$.

Differential entropy extends to continuous distributions: $h(X) = -\int p(x)\log p(x)\,dx$. Unlike discrete entropy, differential entropy can be negative. Among all distributions with fixed variance, the Gaussian maximises differential entropy — the formal reason why Gaussian noise is the "worst case" in many signal processing settings.

📐 Max Entropy Principle
$$h(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma^2) = \max_{p:\,\text{Var}(p)=\sigma^2} h(p)$$

Rényi entropy $H_\alpha(X) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\log\sum_x p(x)^\alpha$ generalises Shannon entropy (recovered at $\alpha\to1$). $H_0$ is the log of the support size, $H_\infty = -\log\max_x p(x)$ (min-entropy). Rényi entropies are used in quantum information and differential privacy ($\varepsilon$-differential privacy uses max-divergence, the Rényi divergence of order $\infty$).

📐 Rényi Entropy & Min-Entropy
$$H_\alpha(X) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\log\!\sum_x p(x)^\alpha \quad H_\infty = -\log\max_x p(x)$$
Concept 02 / 04
📉Cross-Entropy & KL Divergence

Cross-entropy $H(p,q)$ measures the average number of bits needed to encode events from distribution $p$ using a code optimised for distribution $q$. If $q = p$ (perfect model), it equals entropy $H(p)$. If $q \neq p$ (imperfect model), it's always larger — the extra bits wasted are the KL divergence.

This is exactly what happens during training: $p$ is the true label distribution (one-hot), $q$ is your model's softmax output. Cross-entropy loss penalises your model for being "surprised" by the true answer.

💡 Cross-Entropy = Entropy + KL
$H(p,q) = H(p) + D_{KL}(p\|q)$. Since the true labels are fixed, $H(p)$ is constant during training. So minimising cross-entropy is identical to minimising KL divergence between the true distribution and your model. Your model is learning to "think like" the true data distribution.

🧩 Proof Walker: Why Cross-Entropy IS the Right Loss
Step 1 of 4
Maximum Likelihood Estimation
We want model parameters θ that make the observed data most probable. For a dataset of n examples:
$$\hat\theta = \arg\max_\theta \prod_{i=1}^n q_\theta(y_i | x_i)$$
Maximising the product of probabilities — each label $y_i$ should be highly probable under our model $q_\theta$.
Step 2 of 4
Take the log (log-likelihood)
Products become sums under log. Since log is monotone, maximising log-likelihood = maximising likelihood:
$$\arg\max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^n \log q_\theta(y_i|x_i) = \arg\min_\theta -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log q_\theta(y_i|x_i)$$
Step 3 of 4
Recognise the cross-entropy
With one-hot labels, $p(y|x)$ is 1 for the correct class and 0 for others. The negative log-likelihood is exactly the cross-entropy:
$$-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log q_\theta(y_i|x_i) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y\sim p}[-\log q_\theta(y|x)] = H(p, q_\theta)$$
Step 4 of 4 ✓
Cross-entropy minimisation = KL minimisation
Since $H(p,q) = H(p) + D_{KL}(p\|q)$ and $H(p)$ is fixed (true labels don't change), minimising cross-entropy is equivalent to minimising KL divergence — pulling the model distribution towards the true distribution:
$$\arg\min_\theta H(p, q_\theta) = \arg\min_\theta D_{KL}(p \| q_\theta)$$
Every classification model trained with cross-entropy is implicitly minimising the KL divergence between the true label distribution and its predictions. This is the information-theoretic foundation of neural network training.
🤖 In Data Science
In PyTorch: nn.CrossEntropyLoss() combines softmax + NLL loss, computing $-\sum_c y_c \log\hat{y}_c$. For binary classification it's nn.BCELoss(): $-[y\log\hat{y} + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{y})]$. KL divergence appears in VAEs as the regularisation term penalising the encoder from deviating from the prior $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$. In RLHF, a KL penalty keeps the fine-tuned model from drifting too far from the reference policy.
📐 KL Divergence (Gaussians)
$$D_{KL}(p\|q) = \mathbb{E}_p\!\left[\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right] \geq 0 \quad \text{(always)}$$
$$D_{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)\|\mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)) = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2+(\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2}$$

Forward vs reverse KL: $D_{KL}(p\|q)$ (forward) is infinite when $q(x)=0$ but $p(x)>0$ — it forces $q$ to cover all of $p$'s support, tending to produce over-dispersed (mean-seeking) approximations. $D_{KL}(q\|p)$ (reverse) is zero-forcing, preferring $q$ to concentrate on one mode of $p$. This asymmetry matters hugely for variational inference.

The ELBO (Evidence Lower BOund) in VAEs: $\log p(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x)\|p(z))$. The reconstruction term maximises likelihood; the KL term keeps the encoder close to the prior.

📐 VAE ELBO
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{VAE} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[\log p_\theta(x|z)]}_\text{reconstruction} - \underbrace{D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x)\|p(z))}_\text{regularisation}$$

f-divergences generalise KL: $D_f(p\|q) = \int q(x)f(p(x)/q(x))dx$ for a convex function $f$ with $f(1)=0$. KL uses $f(t)=t\log t$, reverse-KL uses $f(t)=-\log t$, total variation uses $f(t)=\frac{1}{2}|t-1|$. The Jensen-Shannon divergence $\text{JSD}(p\|q) = \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(p\|m)+\frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(q\|m)$ where $m=\frac{p+q}{2}$ is symmetric and bounded — it's the loss minimised by the original GAN (binary cross-entropy in the discriminator).

📐 GAN = JSD Minimisation
$$\min_G\max_D V(D,G) = \mathbb{E}_p[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_q[\log(1-D(G(z)))]$$
$$\text{Optimal } D^*(x) = \frac{p(x)}{p(x)+q(x)} \implies V(D^*,G) = 2\,\text{JSD}(p\|q) - \log 4$$
Concept 03 / 04
🔗Mutual Information — Shared Knowledge

Mutual Information $I(X;Y)$ measures how much knowing $X$ tells you about $Y$. It's zero when $X$ and $Y$ are independent — knowing one gives you nothing about the other. It equals $H(Y)$ when $X$ completely determines $Y$ — knowing one tells you everything.

Unlike correlation, mutual information captures any statistical dependency — not just linear ones. Two variables can have zero correlation but high mutual information (e.g. $Y = X^2$).

💡 The Venn Diagram
$I(X;Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y)$. Think of it as the overlap between two circles — the entropy "used by" both variables. If the circles don't overlap (independent), MI = 0. If one circle is inside the other (one determines the other), MI = H(the smaller).

🤖 In Data Science
MI is used for feature selection — pick features with high MI with the label. Information bottleneck theory explains deep learning: networks compress the input (reduce $I(X;\text{representation})$) while preserving task-relevant information ($I(\text{representation};Y)$). MINE (Mutual Information Neural Estimation) estimates MI from samples using neural networks — enabling MI-based representation learning objectives like in CURL and CPC.
📐 Mutual Information
$$I(X;Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y) = D_{KL}(p(x,y)\|p(x)p(y))$$

The data processing inequality: if $X \to Y \to Z$ is a Markov chain, then $I(X;Z) \leq I(X;Y)$. Processing data can only lose information, never gain it. This formalises the idea that no transformation can extract more about X from Z than was already in Y — a fundamental limit on what deep networks can do.

Channel capacity $C = \max_{p(x)} I(X;Y)$ is the maximum information that can reliably be transmitted through a noisy channel. Shannon's channel coding theorem: reliable communication at rate $R$ is possible iff $R < C$. This is why your WiFi connection has a maximum throughput.

📐 Data Processing Inequality
$$X \to Y \to Z \implies I(X;Z) \leq \min(I(X;Y), I(Y;Z))$$

The Information Bottleneck (IB) framework formalises the compression-prediction tradeoff: $\min_{p(\tilde{x}|x)} I(X;\tilde{X}) - \beta I(\tilde{X};Y)$. The IB curve traces Pareto-optimal representations — for each level of compression, the representation that retains the most task-relevant information. Tishby et al. (2017) controversially claimed deep networks implicitly implement IB; this remains contested but highly influential.

📐 Information Bottleneck
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{IB} = I(X;\tilde{X}) - \beta I(\tilde{X};Y) \quad \text{(compress input, preserve label info)}$$
Concept 04 / 04
🌳Information Gain & Decision Trees

Information gain measures how much a feature reduces entropy in the target variable. It's the mutual information between the feature and the label — and it's exactly what decision trees use to choose which feature to split on at each node.

A perfect split creates completely pure child nodes (entropy = 0). A useless split leaves entropy unchanged. The tree greedily picks the feature with the highest information gain at each step.

📐 Information Gain
$$\text{IG}(Y, X) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) = I(X;Y)$$
$$H(Y|X) = \sum_x p(x)\,H(Y|X=x)$$
💡 Worked Example
You have 10 sick/healthy patients and want to split on a test. Before the test, entropy = 1 bit (5/5 split, maximum uncertainty). After a perfect test, each group is entirely sick or healthy — entropy = 0 in both groups. Information gain = 1 bit. The test told you everything.
🤖 In Data Science
Random forests and gradient boosted trees (XGBoost, LightGBM) all use information-theoretic splitting criteria. Feature importance in tree models is literally the total information gain attributed to each feature across all splits. This gives you an interpretable, theoretically grounded measure of feature relevance — unlike correlation, it captures non-linear relationships.

Gini impurity vs entropy: Gini $= 1 - \sum_c p_c^2$ is a second-order Taylor approximation to entropy ($-p\log p \approx p(1-p)$ for small p). They produce nearly identical splits in practice. Entropy is slower to compute (requires log) but slightly favours balanced splits. Most frameworks default to Gini for speed.

📐 Gini ≈ Entropy (Taylor expansion)
$$-p\log p \approx p(1-p) \implies H \approx \text{Gini} \quad \text{for small } p$$

MDL (Minimum Description Length) principle: the best model is the one that most compresses the data. $\text{MDL} = L(\text{model}) + L(\text{data}|\text{model})$ — bits to describe the model plus bits to describe the data given the model. This formalises Occam's razor and connects information theory to Bayesian model selection (the MDL criterion is equivalent to MAP estimation with a prior proportional to $2^{-L(\text{model})}$).

📐 MDL = Bayesian MAP
$$\arg\min_\theta L(\theta) + L(D|\theta) = \arg\max_\theta \log p(\theta) + \log p(D|\theta)$$

Question 1 of 5
A fair coin has higher entropy than a biased coin that lands heads 90% of the time. Why?
A
Because the fair coin is harder to flip
B
Because the outcome is more uncertain — there is more average "surprise" in the fair coin's distribution
C
Because 0.5 is a larger probability than 0.9
D
Because the fair coin has more possible outcomes
✅ Correct! Entropy measures uncertainty/surprise. The fair coin is maximally uncertain — you have no idea what's coming. The biased coin is less surprising because you can guess heads and be right 90% of the time. Entropy peaks at maximum uncertainty.
❌ The answer is B. Entropy measures average surprise. A fair coin is maximally unpredictable — its outcome gives you the most information. A heavily biased coin is predictable — guessing the likely outcome is almost always right, so there's little surprise.
Question 2 of 5
Training a neural network with cross-entropy loss is mathematically equivalent to which of these?
A
Maximising the L2 distance between predictions and labels
B
Minimising mean squared error on the softmax outputs
C
Maximum likelihood estimation — making the true labels most probable under the model
D
Minimising the variance of the model's predictions
✅ Exactly right! Cross-entropy loss is the negative log-likelihood. Minimising it is the same as maximising the probability assigned to the correct labels — which is exactly MLE. The proof walker above derived this step by step.
❌ Cross-entropy is MLE (C). The negative log-likelihood of the true labels under the model's predictions equals the cross-entropy. Minimising it makes the model assign higher probability to the correct answers — which is maximum likelihood estimation.
Question 3 of 5
KL divergence D_KL(p‖q) is zero only when p = q, and always ≥ 0. What does D_KL(p‖q) = 0.5 mean?
A
The model q is 50% accurate
B
Using model q to encode data from p wastes 0.5 extra bits per sample compared to the optimal code
C
p and q overlap by 50%
D
The correlation between p and q is 0.5
✅ Correct! KL divergence is measured in bits (when using log base 2). D_KL = 0.5 means your model q is inefficient by 0.5 bits per sample compared to knowing the true distribution p. This is the information-theoretic interpretation of model error.
❌ KL divergence (B) measures extra bits wasted by using the wrong distribution. D_KL(p‖q) = 0.5 means your model q requires 0.5 more bits per sample than the optimal code (based on p). It's a measure of coding inefficiency, not overlap or accuracy.
Question 4 of 5
Mutual information I(X;Y) = 0 tells you what about X and Y?
A
X and Y are negatively correlated
B
X and Y are statistically independent — knowing X tells you nothing about Y
C
X and Y have the same distribution
D
X completely determines Y
✅ Exactly! I(X;Y) = 0 means the variables are statistically independent. Observing X gives you zero information about Y. In feature selection, a feature with I(feature; label) = 0 is completely useless for prediction — regardless of any linear or nonlinear relationship.
❌ MI = 0 means statistical independence (B). Knowing X gives you no information about Y whatsoever. Note this is stronger than zero correlation — MI = 0 means absolutely no dependency of any kind, linear or nonlinear.
Question 5 of 5
In a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), the KL divergence term in the ELBO objective penalises what?
A
Large reconstruction errors in the decoded output
B
The encoder's latent distribution deviating from the prior N(0,I) — keeping the latent space structured
C
The decoder outputting blurry images
D
Overfitting to the training set
✅ Correct! The KL term D_KL(q_φ(z|x) ‖ p(z)) penalises the encoder for producing a latent distribution that differs from the standard Gaussian prior. This keeps the latent space smooth and continuous — enabling interpolation and random sampling at inference time.
❌ The KL term (B) regularises the encoder's posterior toward the prior N(0,I). Without it, the encoder could map each input to a completely separate region of latent space with no overlap — making interpolation impossible and breaking the "variational" part of VAE. The reconstruction term handles decoding quality.
