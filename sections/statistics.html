Concept 01 / 04
🎲 What is Probability?

Probability is just a number between 0 and 1 that measures how likely something is to happen. Zero means impossible. One means certain. Everything else falls in between.

Think of flipping a fair coin. You can't know in advance whether it'll land heads or tails — but over many flips, heads will come up roughly half the time. That "half" is the probability: 0.5.

💡 Intuition First
Probability is nature's way of being honest about what it doesn't know yet. It's not a flaw in our knowledge — it's a precise mathematical language for describing uncertainty.
🤖 In Data Science
A logistic regression classifier doesn't just say "this email is spam" — it says "this email is spam with probability 0.94." That number is a probability, and knowing how to interpret and calibrate it is fundamental.
📐 Formal Definition
$$P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{Total number of outcomes}}$$

For continuous events, we use integrals: $P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ where $f(x)$ is the probability density function.

Probability theory is built on three axioms (Kolmogorov, 1933): non-negativity $P(A) \geq 0$, normalisation $P(\Omega) = 1$, and additivity $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$ when A and B are mutually exclusive.

From these, everything else is derived. Conditional probability $P(A|B) = P(A \cap B) / P(B)$ measures the probability of A given we know B has occurred.

Independence means knowing B tells you nothing about A: $P(A|B) = P(A)$, equivalently $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$. The i.i.d. (independent, identically distributed) assumption in ML relies directly on this.

💡 Key Insight
Conditional probability is the workhorse of ML. Every time a neural network conditions on input X to predict Y, it's computing P(Y|X). The entire field is essentially learning the best approximation of this conditional distribution.
📐 Kolmogorov Axioms
$$P(A) \geq 0 \quad P(\Omega) = 1 \quad P\!\left(\bigcup_{i} A_i\right) = \sum_i P(A_i)$$

At the pro level, probability theory becomes measure theory. A probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ consists of a sample space $\Omega$, a sigma-algebra $\mathcal{F}$ of measurable events, and a probability measure $P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$.

The Radon-Nikodym theorem formalises the notion of density: if $P \ll \mu$ (P is absolutely continuous w.r.t. $\mu$), there exists an almost-everywhere unique measurable function $f = dP/d\mu$ called the Radon-Nikodym derivative, which generalises the notion of PDF.

In deep learning, this underpins variational inference — we approximate an intractable posterior $p(z|x)$ by minimising KL divergence, which itself is defined in terms of Radon-Nikodym derivatives.

📐 Measure-Theoretic Probability
$$\text{Probability Space: } (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \quad \text{where } P: \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$$
$$\text{Radon-Nikodym: } \frac{dP}{d\mu}(x) = f(x) \implies P(A) = \int_A f(x)\,d\mu(x)$$
Concept 02 / 04
📈 The Normal Distribution

The normal distribution — also called the Gaussian or "bell curve" — is the single most important distribution in all of statistics. It describes the distribution of countless real-world phenomena: heights, test scores, measurement errors, and most averages you'll ever compute.

It's completely described by just two numbers: μ (mean) — where the centre of the bell sits — and σ (standard deviation) — how wide or narrow the bell is.

💡 Intuition
Drag the sliders below. Notice how μ slides the whole curve left and right, while σ stretches or squishes it. A small σ means values cluster tightly around the mean. A large σ means high spread — more uncertainty.

🤖 In Data Science
Features are often assumed Gaussian (Gaussian Naive Bayes, LDA). Residuals in linear regression should be approximately normal. Weight initialisations in neural networks often use $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. The normal distribution is everywhere.
📐 Normal PDF
$$f(x;\mu,\sigma) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

The 68-95-99.7 rule: $P(\mu - \sigma \leq X \leq \mu + \sigma) \approx 0.6827$, etc.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) explains why the normal distribution is so ubiquitous: given i.i.d. random variables $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the standardised sample mean converges in distribution to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ as $n \to \infty$.

This is remarkable: it doesn't matter what distribution the individual $X_i$'s come from. Averages of many independent quantities are always approximately normal. This justifies using normal-distribution-based tests even on non-normal data, provided the sample size is large enough.

📐 Central Limit Theorem
$$\sqrt{n}\,\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$
🤖 In Data Science
The CLT justifies why confidence intervals for model performance metrics (accuracy, AUC) are approximately normally distributed — even when individual predictions are Bernoulli (0 or 1). This underpins statistical hypothesis testing for A/B tests in industry.

The multivariate normal $\mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$ generalises to $d$ dimensions. The covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ encodes pairwise linear dependencies. Its eigendecomposition $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = Q\Lambda Q^T$ reveals the principal axes of the distribution — which is precisely what PCA computes.

The moment generating function $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}$ uniquely characterises the normal and makes computing moments trivial via differentiation.

📐 Multivariate Normal
$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$
Concept 03 / 04
🎯 Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' theorem is the most powerful idea in statistics. It tells you how to update your beliefs when you see new evidence. You start with a "prior" belief, see some data, and compute an updated "posterior" belief.

A classic example: a medical test is 99% accurate. You test positive. What are the chances you're actually sick? Gut feeling says "99%." Bayes says it depends heavily on how rare the disease is — the prior probability. If only 1 in 1000 people have the disease, even a positive test likely means you're fine.

💡 The Formula in Plain English
Posterior = (How likely the evidence if hypothesis is true) × (Prior belief) ÷ (How likely the evidence overall)
🧩 Proof Walker: Deriving Bayes' Theorem
Step 1 of 4
Start with the definition of conditional probability
We know how to write the conditional probability of A given B has occurred:
$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$
This is our starting point. It simply says: the probability of A given B is the fraction of B's probability that overlaps with A.
Step 2 of 4
Write the same for the reverse direction
By the exact same logic, we can write P(B|A):
$$P(B|A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$$
Notice that $P(A \cap B)$ appears in both — the intersection of A and B is the same regardless of which way we look at it. This is the key link.
Step 3 of 4
Solve for the shared term P(A ∩ B)
Rearrange both equations to isolate $P(A \cap B)$:
$$P(A \cap B) = P(A|B)\cdot P(B) = P(B|A)\cdot P(A)$$
Setting these equal gives us the core relationship. We're almost there.
Step 4 of 4 ✓
Rearrange to get Bayes' Theorem
Divide both sides by P(B) to isolate P(A|B):
$$\boxed{P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A)\cdot P(A)}{P(B)}}$$
Done! In ML terms: Posterior = Likelihood × Prior / Evidence. This is the entire foundation of Bayesian inference.

🤖 In Data Science
Naive Bayes classifiers, Bayesian hyperparameter optimisation, Gaussian processes, and variational autoencoders all build directly on Bayes' theorem. The entire field of Bayesian deep learning uses it to quantify model uncertainty — essential for real-world deployment.

Bayesian inference treats model parameters $\theta$ as random variables. We start with a prior $p(\theta)$, observe data $\mathcal{D}$, and update using the likelihood $p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)$:

📐 Bayesian Inference
$$p(\theta|\mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)\,p(\theta)}{p(\mathcal{D})} \propto p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)\,p(\theta)$$

The denominator $p(\mathcal{D}) = \int p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)p(\theta)d\theta$ is the marginal likelihood or "evidence" — often intractable for complex models. This is why variational inference (ELBO) and MCMC sampling exist.

💡 Conjugate Priors
When prior and posterior have the same functional form, the prior is called "conjugate." E.g., Beta prior + Bernoulli likelihood = Beta posterior. This gives closed-form updates — no integration needed. Used extensively in online learning and Thompson sampling for bandit algorithms.

Variational Bayes approximates the intractable posterior $p(\theta|\mathcal{D})$ by finding a distribution $q(\theta) \in \mathcal{Q}$ minimising $\text{KL}(q\|p)$. This is equivalent to maximising the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

📐 ELBO Derivation
$$\log p(\mathcal{D}) = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mathcal{D},\theta)] - \mathbb{E}_q[\log q(\theta)] + \text{KL}(q\|p)$$
$$\mathcal{L}(q) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_q[\log p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)]}_{\text{reconstruction}} - \underbrace{\text{KL}(q(\theta)\|p(\theta))}_{\text{regularisation}}$$

This is the VAE objective. The reparameterisation trick $\theta = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ makes it differentiable w.r.t. $\mu, \sigma$, enabling backpropagation through the stochastic latent variable.

Concept 04 / 04
⚖️ Expectation & Variance

The expected value (or mean) $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is the long-run average of a random variable. If you repeat an experiment infinitely many times, the average outcome converges to the expectation.

The variance $\text{Var}(X)$ measures spread — how far values typically deviate from the mean. Its square root is the standard deviation σ, which is in the same units as X.

💡 Intuition
Two data scientists both predict a test score of 75. One always predicts exactly 75 (zero variance). The other's predictions vary wildly between 50 and 100. Same expectation, wildly different variance — and wildly different reliability.
📐 Expectation & Variance
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x x\,P(X=x) \quad \text{(discrete)}$$
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\,f(x)\,dx \quad \text{(continuous)}$$
$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$
🤖 In Data Science
The mean squared error loss is $\mathbb{E}[(y - \hat{y})^2]$. Batch normalisation normalises activations by their mean and variance. The bias-variance tradeoff describes the tension between $\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)]$ and $\text{Var}(\hat{f}(x))$ — two quantities that determine your model's generalisation error.

Linearity of expectation: $\mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = a\mathbb{E}[X] + b\mathbb{E}[Y]$ — even if X and Y are dependent. This is one of the most useful facts in probability.

For variance: $\text{Var}(aX + bY) = a^2\text{Var}(X) + b^2\text{Var}(Y) + 2ab\,\text{Cov}(X,Y)$. The covariance term disappears only when X and Y are uncorrelated.

The bias-variance decomposition splits expected prediction error into irreducible noise, squared bias, and variance — formalising the tradeoff between underfitting and overfitting.

📐 Bias-Variance Decomposition
$$\mathbb{E}[(y - \hat{f})^2] = \underbrace{\text{Bias}^2(\hat{f})}_{\text{underfitting}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}(\hat{f})}_{\text{overfitting}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2_\epsilon}_{\text{irreducible}}$$

Concentration inequalities bound how far a random variable can deviate from its expectation with high probability. Markov's inequality gives a loose bound; Chebyshev tightens it using variance; Hoeffding (for bounded RVs) and Chernoff (for exponential moments) give exponentially tight bounds.

These are the backbone of PAC learning theory — they bound generalisation error as a function of sample size $n$ and hypothesis class complexity (VC dimension, Rademacher complexity).

📐 Hoeffding's Inequality
$$P\!\left(\bar{X} - \mu \geq t\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{2n^2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n(b_i-a_i)^2}\right)$$
