The Geometry
of Data

Data science is fundamentally about transforming high-dimensional data. Linear algebra is the language of those transformations — the mathematical toolkit that makes neural networks, PCA, and attention mechanisms possible.

Why does a data scientist need this?
A neural network is a sequence of matrix multiplications. PCA is eigendecomposition. Attention is dot products. SVD powers recommender systems. Every single core ML operation is linear algebra under the hood — without it, you can't read a paper, debug a model, or understand what your code is actually doing.

Concept 01 / 04
↗️Vectors — Data as Arrows

A vector is an ordered list of numbers. That's it. But there's a powerful geometric interpretation: you can think of a vector as an arrow in space, pointing in a specific direction with a specific length.

In data science, a single data point — say, a person with height=175, weight=70, age=28 — is a vector in 3-dimensional space: [175, 70, 28]. Every row in your dataset is a point (vector) in high-dimensional space.

💡 Geometric Intuition
Two vectors are similar if they point in roughly the same direction. This is the geometric heart of cosine similarity, attention mechanisms, and embedding spaces — things you use every day in NLP models.
🤖 In Data Science
Word embeddings (Word2Vec, BERT) represent words as vectors. Recommendation systems represent users and items as vectors. The "closer" two vectors are in space, the more similar the concepts they represent. This is why vector databases power modern RAG systems.
📐 Vector Operations
$$\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n\end{bmatrix} \quad \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2}$$
$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i = \|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$$

A vector space $V$ over field $\mathbb{F}$ is a set closed under addition and scalar multiplication, satisfying 8 axioms. The key structures: subspaces (subsets that are themselves vector spaces), spans (all linear combinations of a set of vectors), and linear independence (no vector is a linear combination of the others).

A basis is a minimal spanning set — it's both spanning and linearly independent. The number of basis vectors is the dimension of the space.

📐 Dot Product & Cosine Similarity
$$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\|\mathbf{v}\|} \in [-1, 1]$$
🤖 In Data Science
The attention score $\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d_k})V$ is literally dot products between query and key vectors, scaled and normalised. Understanding vector spaces makes transformer architectures immediately readable.

In the pro view, we consider dual spaces $V^*$ (the space of linear functionals on $V$), tensor products $V \otimes W$, and norms that define the geometry of the space. Different norms ($L_1$, $L_2$, $L_\infty$) induce different notions of distance and have different regularisation properties.

The $L_1$ norm (LASSO regularisation) promotes sparsity because its unit ball has corners on the axes. The $L_2$ norm (Ridge) spreads the penalty evenly. Understanding why requires knowing the geometry of these normed vector spaces.

📐 Norms & Regularisation
$$\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 = \sum|w_i|,\quad \|\mathbf{w}\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum w_i^2},\quad \|\mathbf{w}\|_\infty = \max|w_i|$$
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{LASSO} = \text{MSE} + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|_1 \qquad \mathcal{L}_\text{Ridge} = \text{MSE} + \lambda\|\mathbf{w}\|_2^2$$

🔄Matrices as Transformations

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers — but the real insight is what it does: a matrix is a linear transformation. It takes vectors as input and outputs transformed vectors.

Multiplying a vector by a 2×2 matrix rotates, scales, shears, or reflects it. This is literally what happens inside every layer of a neural network: your input vector gets multiplied by a weight matrix, producing a new vector.

🤖 In Data Science
In a neural network layer: output = activation(W·x + b). W is a matrix. Multiplying by W transforms the input vector into a new representation space. Deep networks chain these transformations, each one learning to detect increasingly abstract features.
📐 Matrix Multiplication
$$(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n A_{ik} B_{kj}$$
$$\begin{bmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}ax+by \\ cx+dy\end{bmatrix}$$

Key matrix properties for ML: The rank of a matrix is the dimension of its column space — a rank-deficient matrix means some features are redundant. The determinant measures how much the transformation scales area/volume ($\det = 0$ means the matrix collapses space into lower dimensions).

Matrix inverses $A^{-1}$ exist only for square, full-rank matrices. Pseudoinverses $A^+$ generalise this for overdetermined or underdetermined systems — they give the least-squares solution, underpinning linear regression.

📐 Linear Regression via Pseudoinverse
$$\hat{\boldsymbol\beta} = (X^TX)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y} = X^+\mathbf{y}$$

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD): Any $m \times n$ matrix $A$ decomposes as $A = U\Sigma V^T$, where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal matrices (rotations) and $\Sigma$ is diagonal (scalings). This reveals the fundamental geometry of any linear map.

Truncated SVD (keeping only the top $k$ singular values) gives the best rank-$k$ approximation in the Frobenius norm sense — this is the Eckart-Young theorem and is the theoretical foundation of both PCA and low-rank matrix factorisation for recommenders.

📐 SVD & Low-Rank Approximation
$$A = U\Sigma V^T = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T$$
$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i \mathbf{u}_i\mathbf{v}_i^T = \underset{\text{rank}(B)\leq k}{\arg\min}\|A-B\|_F$$
Concept 03 / 04
↗️Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors

Most vectors get knocked off their original direction when you multiply them by a matrix. But some special vectors — called eigenvectors — only get scaled, not rotated. The amount they scale by is the eigenvalue.

Think of it this way: if you stretch a rubber sheet and watch which directions of marks stay pointing in the same direction (just longer or shorter), those are the eigenvectors. The stretch factor is the eigenvalue.

💡 The Key Equation
$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ — applying the matrix A to eigenvector v just scales it by λ. The direction doesn't change. This is extraordinary: it means we've found the "natural axes" of the transformation.

🧩 Proof Walker: How to Find Eigenvalues
Step 1 of 4
Start with the eigenvector definition
We want to find non-zero vectors v and scalars λ such that:
$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$$
This says: multiplying v by A gives the same result as scaling v by λ. The direction of v doesn't change.
Step 2 of 4
Rearrange into a homogeneous system
Move everything to one side:
$$A\mathbf{v} - \lambda\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \implies (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$$
For this to have a non-zero solution v, the matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ must be singular — it must collapse space into lower dimensions.
Step 3 of 4
Use the determinant condition
A matrix is singular if and only if its determinant is zero:
$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$
This is the characteristic equation. Expanding the determinant gives a polynomial in λ. The roots of this polynomial are the eigenvalues.
Step 4 of 4 ✓
Solve for eigenvalues, then eigenvectors
For a 2×2 matrix $\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}$:
$$\det\begin{bmatrix}a-\lambda & b \\ c & d-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (a-\lambda)(d-\lambda)-bc = 0$$
Solve this quadratic for λ₁, λ₂. Then plug each back into $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}$ to find the eigenvectors.
🤖 In Data Science
PCA finds the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix. The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue is the direction of maximum variance — the first principal component. Google's PageRank is the leading eigenvector of the web's link matrix. Markov chains converge to their eigenvector (stationary distribution).

Spectral decomposition: A symmetric matrix $A$ can be decomposed as $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, where $Q$ is orthogonal (columns are eigenvectors) and $\Lambda$ is diagonal (eigenvalues). This is the eigendecomposition, which PCA relies on entirely.

The trace equals the sum of eigenvalues ($\text{tr}(A) = \sum\lambda_i$) and the determinant equals their product ($\det(A) = \prod\lambda_i$). This gives geometric meaning: det=0 means some eigenvalue is zero, meaning the transformation collapses space to lower dimensions.

📐 Spectral Decomposition
$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$$

The Perron-Frobenius theorem guarantees that for a non-negative irreducible matrix, there is a unique largest real eigenvalue (the Perron root) with a corresponding strictly positive eigenvector. This underpins PageRank, Markov chain stationary distributions, and spectral clustering.

Power iteration computes the leading eigenvector in $O(n^2)$ per iteration — vastly more efficient than full eigendecomposition for large sparse matrices, which is why it scales to web-scale graphs.

📐 Power Iteration
$$\mathbf{v}^{(t+1)} = \frac{A\mathbf{v}^{(t)}}{\|A\mathbf{v}^{(t)}\|} \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \mathbf{q}_1 \text{ (leading eigenvector)}$$
Concept 04 / 04
🌐PCA — Dimensionality Reduction

Your dataset has 100 features. Most of them are correlated. PCA (Principal Component Analysis) finds new axes — called principal components — along which the data varies the most. It then projects your data onto the top few components, reducing dimensions while preserving most of the information.

It's like finding the best angle to photograph a 3D object so the 2D photo captures as much detail as possible.

💡 PCA = Eigenvectors of Covariance
The principal components are the eigenvectors of the data's covariance matrix. The first PC is the eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue — the direction of maximum spread. Drag the data cloud below and watch how the PC axis follows the direction of maximum variance.

🤖 In Data Science
PCA is used for: visualising high-dimensional data (project to 2D for plotting), reducing input dimensions before training (speeds up models, reduces overfitting), removing multicollinearity in regression, and compressing images. The "explained variance ratio" tells you how much information each component retains.
📐 PCA Algorithm
$$\Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1}X^TX \quad\text{(covariance matrix)}$$
$$\Sigma = Q\Lambda Q^T \quad\text{(eigendecompose)}$$
$$Z = XQ_k \quad\text{(project to top-}k\text{ components)}$$

PCA maximises variance: the first PC is $\mathbf{w}_1 = \arg\max_{\|\mathbf{w}\|=1} \mathbf{w}^T\Sigma\mathbf{w}$. By the Lagrangian, this gives $\Sigma\mathbf{w}_1 = \lambda_1\mathbf{w}_1$ — confirming PC₁ is the leading eigenvector of the covariance matrix.

The fraction of variance explained by component $k$ is $\lambda_k / \sum_i \lambda_i$. The "elbow" in a scree plot shows where adding more components gives diminishing returns.

📐 Variance Explained
$$\text{Explained Variance Ratio}_k = \frac{\lambda_k}{\sum_{i=1}^d \lambda_i}$$

PCA is equivalent to SVD of the centred data matrix: $X = U\Sigma V^T \Rightarrow$ PCs are columns of $V$, scores are columns of $U\Sigma$. This is computationally preferable for tall/skinny matrices.

Kernel PCA extends PCA to nonlinear manifolds by implicitly mapping to a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) via the kernel trick $k(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x}') = \phi(\mathbf{x})^T\phi(\mathbf{x}')$, then performing PCA in that space without ever computing $\phi$ explicitly.

📐 Kernel PCA
$$K_{ij} = k(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) \quad \tilde{K} = K - \mathbf{1}_n K - K\mathbf{1}_n + \mathbf{1}_n K \mathbf{1}_n$$
$$\text{Eigendecompose } \tilde{K} \text{ to get nonlinear principal components}$$

🔑 Key Terms
Span
All possible linear combinations of a set of vectors.
Rank
Dimension of the column space — number of linearly independent columns.
Orthogonal
Vectors at 90° (dot product = 0). Orthogonal matrices preserve lengths.
Positive Definite
All eigenvalues > 0. Covariance matrices are always PSD (≥ 0).

Question 3 of 5
PCA selects the principal components by finding the eigenvectors of which matrix?
A
The weight matrix W of a neural network
B
The data matrix X directly
C
The covariance matrix Σ = XᵀX / (n−1)
D

✅ Correct! PCA eigendecomposes the covariance matrix Σ. The eigenvectors are the principal components, and the eigenvalues tell you how much variance each component captures.
❌ PCA finds eigenvectors of the covariance matrix Σ = XᵀX/(n−1) — answer C. The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue is the direction of maximum variance in the data.
Question 4 of 5
What does a determinant of zero mean geometrically for a matrix transformation?
A
The transformation is a pure rotation
B
The transformation squashes space to a lower dimension (not invertible)
C
The transformation doubles all areas
D
All eigenvalues are equal to 1
✅ Right! Det = 0 means the transformation collapses space to a lower dimension — like squishing a 2D plane onto a line. The matrix is singular and has no inverse.
❌ A determinant of zero (B) means the transformation collapses space — it's singular, not invertible. At least one eigenvalue must be zero. This is why covariance matrices must be full-rank for PCA to work properly.
Question 5 of 5
Cosine similarity between two word embedding vectors is used to measure what?
A
The Euclidean distance between the words in the vocabulary
B
The angle between the vectors — how similar the words are in meaning
C
The number of shared characters between the words
D
The magnitude of the word frequency in the corpus
✅ Exactly! Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors, not their magnitude. Two semantically similar words (like "king" and "queen") will have vectors pointing in similar directions, giving cosine similarity close to 1.
❌ Cosine similarity (B) measures the angle between two vectors — how aligned their directions are. In word embeddings, similar words point in similar directions, so cos θ ≈ 1 for synonyms regardless of how long the vectors are.
